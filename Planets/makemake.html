Makemake: The Elusive Dwarf Planet

Makemake is a dwarf planet located in the Kuiper Belt, a region beyond Neptune filled with icy bodies. It is one of the largest known dwarf planets and was discovered in 2005. Makemake is named after the creation deity of the Rapa Nui people of Easter Island.

Key Facts About Makemake:

  • Distance from the Sun: ~6.85 billion km (4.25 billion miles)
  • Diameter: ~1,430 km (890 miles)
  • Length of a Day: ~22.5 hours
  • Length of a Year: ~305 Earth years
  • Gravity: ~0.05 times Earth's gravity
  • Temperature Range: -240°C (-400°F)

Surface and Composition:

Makemake has a bright, icy surface primarily made of methane, ethane, and possibly nitrogen ice. Its reddish hue is similar to Pluto’s, but it lacks a significant atmosphere. Observations suggest its surface may be covered in frozen methane, giving it a reflective appearance.

Atmosphere and Climate:

Unlike Pluto, Makemake does not appear to have a significant atmosphere. However, some scientists believe that it may develop a temporary atmosphere when it gets closer to the Sun, as frozen gases sublimate. Its surface remains extremely cold, with temperatures reaching as low as -240°C.

Orbital Speed and Rotation:

Makemake orbits the Sun at an average speed of 4.4 km/s (2.7 miles/s). A day on Makemake lasts about 22.5 hours, making its rotation similar to Earth’s. However, its year is about 305 Earth years long, meaning it takes a very long time to complete one orbit around the Sun.

Exploration:

Makemake has not yet been visited by any spacecraft. Most of what we know about this dwarf planet comes from telescopic observations, including those made by the Hubble Space Telescope. Future missions could provide more details about its surface and composition.

Fun Facts About Makemake:

  • Makemake was discovered on March 31, 2005, by astronomers at the Palomar Observatory.
  • It was named after the creator god of the Rapa Nui people from Easter Island.
  • Makemake is one of the brightest objects in the Kuiper Belt, second only to Pluto.
  • It has one known moon, discovered in 2016, though little is known about it.
